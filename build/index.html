A guide to digital design at The Guardian

06/02/18

Introduction The Guardian website is made up of a series of fronts and articles.
Introduction Fronts are broken down into containers — these allow stories to be organised in a logical manner.
Introduction Containers are composed of modular blocks of story cards. These are designed to be flexible; they expand and contract depending on the number of stories added to them.
Introduction Cards can hold an image, kicker, headline, standfirst and meta information.
  • Kicker — Guardian Headline Bold
  • Headline — Guardian Headline Medium
  • Standfirst — Guardian Text Egyptian
  • Meta — Guardian Text Sans
Introduction We have a variety of different card sizes which create hierarchy and pace on the page which allows us to quickly indicate a stories importance.
Introduction This is a selection of the card types. Each card type is designed to give a sense of the tone of the story.
Introduction This is a selection of the article types. Articles follow a similar design language to the cards, each having its own distinct flavour and tone.
Introduction Thrashers are custom designed containers usually promoting single articles or a series.
Grids & spacing The grid unifies the way we arrange information on all of our digital platforms.
Grids & spacing The desktop grid is based on columns of 60px with 20px gutters.
Grids & spacing We use 4 fixed grid sizes on large screens:
  • Wide — 1300px
  • LeftCol — 1140px
  • Desktop — 980px
  • Tablet — 740px
Grids & spacing A fluid grid on mobile devices. With tweak points at:
  • Phablet — 740px
  • MobileLandscape — 480px
  • MobileMedium — 375px
Grids & spacing Spacing is intentionally used to reference the placement of the typography in print.
Grids & spacing The placement of the typography here allows for consistency across print and digital platforms.
Grids & spacing This alignment is consistent on headlines, bylines and container titles across fronts and articles.
Brand use The Guardian has two expressions of its brand.
Brand use The logo and the roundel are always aligned either top right or bottom right.
Brand use The roundel acts as a simplified expression of the brand — often used to mark the Guardian’s own content on- and off-platform. For more, visit The Guardian Brand hub
Typography The Guardian has two core typefaces and three additional typefaces.
  • Guardian Headline (core)
  • Guardian Titlepiece (core)
  • Guardian Text Egyptian
  • Guardian Display Sans
  • Guardian Text Sans
Typography Guardian Headline is designed to allow for the optimal reading experience across all platforms. It is used across the website and app for headlines, giving them a unique character and voice.
Typography Guardian Titlepiece is an accompaniment to The Guardian’s logotype. It is bold and striking so use cautiously and appropriately.
Typography Our display typefaces are matched by highly legible text variants.
Typography We use our sans serif for small sizes or meta information.
Colour palette We have a colour palette that allows us to be bold, playful, tasteful and sensitive.
Colour palette Our colour palette is designed to aid our readers in navigating, therefore consistency is important.
Colour palette Colour is an important tool for both our UI and the editorial that is produced. Colours are used to signify the pillar under which content sits.

Graphic elements We use various devices to create hierarchy, structure and help people navigate
Graphic elements We have two styles of button. Consider weighting when choosing a style: the lozenge is bolder and the circular button is more subtle. Icons are used to help the reader navigate and sit centrally inside an imaginary circle at the edge of the button
Graphic elements We use highlighting to create impact, help aid hierarchy and emphasis within designs
Graphic elements Rules give structure and they also help to add a subtle difference in flavour across articles. We have 4 different types that get used across the website.
